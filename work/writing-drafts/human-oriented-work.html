Human-oriented work in a tools-obsessed moment

Essay · First published here · Adapted from Jenny’s positioning notes

Everyone wants to talk about the tool. Fewer people want to talk about the team around the tool — who owns what, how decisions get made when something breaks, what “good” looks like when the roadmap changes every quarter.

I’ve spent my career on the other side of that imbalance: making programs, campaigns, and experiences that treat people as the point — not as an afterthought in a slide about “change management.”

That doesn’t mean anti-technology. It means technology judged by whether real humans can use it well in the messy reality of their week: competing priorities, unclear ownership, the gap between what demos promise and what teams can sustain.

When I say the best work is human-oriented, I mean experiences worth someone’s time — whether that someone is a customer in a training lab, a new hire on day one, or a room full of practitioners who don’t owe you attention.

I also mean something that sounds soft until you’ve shipped without it: delight is worth leading toward. Not gimmicks — respect. Clarity. The feeling that the people building the thing actually cared whether the audience could follow.

That orientation is the through-line for me — from early-stage GTM to people programs to the unglamorous operational work that keeps a company pointed in one direction. Tools change fast. Teams and taste still show up in everything that lasts.
